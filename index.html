The Method

What Is a Heuristic Evaluation?

A heuristic evaluation is a quick, expert-driven usability inspection. A small group of reviewers examines an interface against a proven set of design principles — no test users required.

The Analogy

It's a building inspector walking through a house before it's sold. They don't live in it — but a trained eye catches the loose railing and faulty wiring long before a resident ever would.

FastLow-cost next to full usability testing
3–5Evaluators — one person misses most problems

The Method

Why Three to Five Evaluators

A single reviewer catches only about a third of usability problems. Each extra evaluator sees issues the others miss — because everyone brings different mental models.

Coverage climbs steeply, then flattens. Around five evaluators you find ~75% of problems — past that, the cost outweighs the catch.

Heuristic 01

Visibility of System Status

Keep users informed with timely, appropriate feedback for every action — a progress bar, a status line, a confirmation.

Analogy

A lift with a floor indicator — you always know where you are. Silence during a booking feels like a stuck elevator.

In IRCTC: no loading indicator after tapping "Book Now" left users unsure the action registered.

Heuristic 02

Match Between System & Real World

Speak the user's language. Use familiar words and real-world conventions instead of system-oriented jargon.

Analogy

A foreign menu with photos beside unfamiliar dishes. Jargon like "GNWL" needs that same visual translation.

Heuristic 03

User Control & Freedom

Give users a clear, low-cost way out of unwanted states — a marked exit, plus undo and redo.

Analogy

An emergency-exit sign: you rarely use it, but its absence turns a small mistake into a panic.

In IRCTC: after selecting the wrong train, there was no quick way back to results.

Heuristic 04

Consistency & Standards

The same word, icon, or action should always mean the same thing. Follow platform conventions.

Analogy

Traffic lights mean the same at every junction. Imagine if red meant "stop" on one street and "go" on the next.

In IRCTC: button styles and terminology differed between mobile and desktop.
Before · inconsistent

Four buttons, four visual languages — users re-learn each one.
After · consistent

One system — shape, size and casing all signal "these are buttons."

Heuristic 06

Recognition Rather Than Recall

Keep options visible. Don't make users memorize information from one step to the next.

Analogy

A printed menu vs. a waiter reciting dishes from memory. Recognizing a listed item is far easier than recalling one.

In IRCTC: users had to recall train and station codes instead of picking from suggestions.

Heuristic 07

Flexibility & Efficiency of Use

Offer shortcuts for experts without cluttering the screen for novices. Let people tailor frequent actions.

Analogy

A camera with "Auto" for beginners and manual dials for pros — both live on one device without getting in each other's way.

Heuristic 09

Help Users Recover from Errors

Errors should be in plain language, precisely state the problem, and constructively suggest a fix.

Analogy

A GPS that says "recalculating route" instead of just "error" — it tells you what went wrong and what happens next.

Heuristic 10

Help & Documentation

When help is needed, make it searchable, task-focused, concise — and right where the user is stuck.

Analogy

A good recipe card, not a culinary textbook — you want the three steps you need, not everything the author knows.

In IRCTC: the help section was hard to find, outdated and not task-oriented.

Prioritization

Reading a Severity Scale

Each issue is scored by frequency, impact and persistence — severity tells the team what to fix first, not just what exists.

1
Cosmetic
Fix only if time allows — minor visual issue.
2
Minor
Low priority — small friction, easy workaround.
3
Moderate
Important to fix — slows many users down.
4
Major
High priority — blocks or frustrates the task.
5
Catastrophe
Must fix before release — users cannot succeed.

Beyond a Single Audit

The Project Design Lifecycle

Heuristic evaluation is one checkpoint in an iterative cycle — usability is built stage by stage, not bolted on at the end.

1
Research
Understand users and pain points
2
Design
Wireframes to high-fidelity UI
3
Prototype
Simulate flows before building
4
Build
Implement with real engineering
5
Evaluate
Test usability, fix what breaks
6
Launch
Release, monitor, and iterate
The cycle loops — launch feedback flows straight back into research for the next release.

Wrapping Up

Key Takeaways

01
Heuristics are a lens, not a checklist
They guide expert judgment — severity and context matter more than a rigid rulebook.
02
Small frictions compound
A missing loading spinner and an unexplained acronym both erode trust in the same session.
03
Usability is a cycle, not a step
Research, design, evaluation and launch feed back into one another continuously.
